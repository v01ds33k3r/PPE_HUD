\subsubsection{Visualizing Oxygen Usage With a Status Bar}

Since the oxygen value is the decisive factor whether or not to continue work, this component takes center stage. As the most important parameter, this component will be visible in any version of the HUD. To ensure the current status is recognizable at a glance, the value is visualized with a status bar.

A radial version of the component was created based on mechanical gauges, whose hands indicate the status by default. This component also occupies a relatively square area within the grid, which allows for alternative layout options.
However, compared to the linear version, it requires significantly more space to ensure the information is legible. Furthermore, as mentioned in section \ref{sec:datavis}, a linear design enables much faster information processing.

\begin{figure}[hbt]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{assets/oxy_radial_bar.eps}
    \captionbelow[Radial and linear status bar]{Radial and linear concepts for the status bar}
    \label{fig:oxy_radial_bar}
\end{figure}

In the second version, the value is displayed to the right below the bar. As the oxygen level decreases, the empty sections of the bar appear only as outlines, which increases the contrast between the value and the bar.
The label is placed in front of the bar so that the two blend together, making the label less noticeable.
In addition, the status bar has been divided into quarter segments, allowing for a more accurate assessment of the remaining amount without having to look at the value.

\begin{figure}[hbt]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{assets/oxy_horizontal.eps}
    \captionbelow[Quarter segmentation of the status bar]{The status bar divided into quarter segments, allowing status assessment without having to look at the value.}
    \label{fig:oxy_segment}
\end{figure}

Depending on the available screen space and the layout configuration, a vertical version of the component should be available in addition to the horizontal one.
Grouping the label and value on the same side saves space. Aligning them at the top and bottom mitigates visual crowding and accentuates the value.
The labels are positioned so that the status bar is perceived first, i.e. when the component is placed on the left side, the bar will be on the right to provide visual guidance.

\begin{figure}[hbt]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{assets/oxy_vertical.eps}
    \captionbelow[Vertical status bar]{Development of the vertical status bar. The correct version accounts for visual crowding and accentuates the value.}
    \label{fig:oxy_vertical}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{The Base Component}

Secondary data—which, while helpful, is not a priority and therefore does not need to be visualized—can be based on a standardized base component. This includes, for example, data such as heart rate, as well as environmental information such as ambient temperature or humidity.

Apart from the actual parameter value, any element of the base component can be hidden. This allows for flexible configuration depending on the type of information. Because they share a common base, the information is visually similar enough to be grouped into blocks.

\begin{figure}[hbt]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{assets/base_component.eps}
    \captionbelow[The base component and example configurations]{The base component with configurable icon, parameter value, unit, and trend indicator, as well as sample configurations for heart rate, tool help, and countdown.}
    \label{fig:base_component}
\end{figure}

The icons replace labels and should therefore be as unambiguous as possible. In some cases, the unit helps identify the value (e.g., degrees Fahrenheit or Celsius). If this is not the case, they should preferably be omitted.

Trend indicators are displayed based on the difference between the current and previous values. An unusually sharp increase or decrease in the value may indicate events that warrant attention. For dynamic elements such as these, the frequency of changes should not be too high to avoid distractions.

All of these values are displayed continuously, with the exception of the time display, which appears only after a certain period of time has elapsed. The appearance of this element in the peripheral vision signals (for example) that half of the time limit has passed, which encodes this action itself with information.

Since the working time is limited by various parameters and is therefore not fixed, the component is formatted as a countdown. To avoid drawing attention to itself with too frequent changes, the display shows only minutes rather than counting down to the exact second.

\subsubsection{Notifications and Pop-Ups}

Referencing conventional industrial warning signs, the hazard icons were created in a triangular and a diamond-shaped version. Comparing them however, reveals that the diamond shape has smaller overall dimensions, which also means there is less space available for the illustration.

To standardize their function, the same silhouette is used for all icons, meaning that their spatial arrangement is much more important for peripheral distinction. Similar to the warning lights on a car's dashboard, each symbol has a fixed position to facilitate rapid information acquisition.

\begin{figure}[hbt]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{assets/icons_hazard.eps}
    \captionbelow[Hazard indicator icons]{Hazard indicator icons for electricity, explosive atmosphere and fire. The triangle and diamond shapes reference warning and hazard signage, respectively.}
    \label{fig:notif}
\end{figure}

The prime example of the notification system is the high-priority, billboard-style alert (see Figure \ref{fig:text_styles}). The large, bright area ensures good visibility even when the pop-up is not in the center of the user's field of view. Since it has the highest priority, it is the only element placed in the upper central area of the screen. To avoid obscuring vision for too long, the pop-up shrinks to a smaller version but continues to flash to remain in the user's awareness.

This occurs without user intervention. Low oxygen levels or hazard proximity do require action, but in the intended scenarios there may be no opportunity to act immediately, and the HUD should not hinder the user's decision-making, which would be the case with a persistent alarm.

\subsubsection{The Checklist Component}

Since the checklist is a text-based element, it is not intended to be comprehensible without looking at it. Nevertheless, some of the previous guidelines can be applied. To draw the user's attention to the current task, it is vertically centered and stands out more clearly from the other tasks on the screen.

\begin{figure}[hbt]
    \centering
    \setlength{\fboxsep}{24pt}
    \setlength{\fboxrule}{2pt}
    \fbox{\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{assets/checklist.eps}}
    \captionbelow[Checklist Component]{Default and expanded checklist. Note the center alignment of the current task to avoid losing focus when the list expands.}
    \label{fig:checklist}
\end{figure}

The icon serves as an anchor for potential gaze-based interaction, for example, to display additional details such as completed tasks and subtasks. The design keeps the current task centered even in the detail view to enable faster orientation. This also mitigates the impact of potential false alarms—i.e., if the system mistakenly opens the detail view.

\subsection{UI Examples}

{\small\emph{Note: the following illustrations are not to scale.}}

The full-featured version of the user interface is based on Microsoft's HoloLens 2, which has a horizontal field of view of 43°, a resolution of 1440 $\times$ 936 pixels per eye, and covers the entire color spectrum and provides full color availability (within the technical limits mentioned in Section \ref{sec:AR}).

\begin{figure}[hbt]
    \centering
    \setlength{\fboxrule}{2pt}
    \fbox{\includegraphics[width=0.97\textwidth]{assets/UI_max.eps}}
    \captionbelow[UI for binocular HUD]{Full-featured UI concept for a binocular HUD.}
    \label{fig:UI_max}
\end{figure}

Although the oxygen component is the most important, it is not necessarily the one that is checked most often—however, it should be the easiest to check. Therefore, it is placed in the lower center. Occasional information pop-ups are placed above the oxygen bar for the same reason: looking down is faster than looking up.

The block containing individual parameters is located on the left side, so that the status indicators are seen first, since they are the primary information of this component. This leaves the right side free for the checklist, which provides visual guidance thanks to its left-aligned text.

\begin{figure}[hbt]
    \centering
    \setlength{\fboxrule}{2pt}
    \fbox{\includegraphics[width=0.97\textwidth]{assets/UI_grid.png}}
    \captionbelow[Grid guide of the full-featured UI]{Grid guide of the full-featured UI}
    \label{fig:UI_grid}
\end{figure}

The minimal version of the user interface is based on the Google Glass Enterprise Edition 2, which features a monocular display with a resolution of 640 $\times$ 360 pixels.
The limited space requires strict prioritization of elements.
To ensure that the information is still easy to take in, all elements should be carefully aligned.
