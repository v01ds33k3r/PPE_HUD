\section{Augmented Reality Technology}
\label{sec:AR}

Augmented reality (AR) is a subcategory of extended reality (XR), next to mixed reality (MR) and virtual reality (VR). In AR virtual objects are "superimposed upon or composited with the real world" [\cite{azumaSurveyAugmentedReality1997}], in opposition to virtual reality which—by replacing the real environment entirely—creates the deepest sense of immersion. Mixed reality combines features of both, the main distinction being the ability to interact with virtual objects in the real world [\cite{harichandanaExploringFrontiersUser2023}].

    \label{fig:XR_spectrum}
\end{figure}

Due to not being constrained to two dimensions, XR applications can explore new types of interaction. These technologies share many aspects of interface design and interaction, though each has advantages and disadvantages of its own.

\textcite{azumaSurveyAugmentedReality1997} defines three requirements for applications to be classified as AR. They combine real and virtual content, they are interactive in real-time, and virtual objects are registered in 3D space. The last criterium was chosen so as to include other technologies besides HMDs. 
- real and virtual objects "coexist" in the same space, thus requires see-through, contrary to VR

A head-up display overlays information onto reality in the user's line of sight to avoid the need to look away from their primary task to acquire needed information. Originally used in military aircraft, today HUDs are built into consumer vehicles as well. For a more mobile use, the HUD can be implemented as a head-mounted display (HMD).

The displayed information is not anchored to the real world. In the case of a head-worn display, the information is head-stabilized, i.e. 2D planes around the user (see figure \ref{fig:XR_layers}). The most important feature of head-stabilized content is that it is in the same location every time.

World-stabilized elements are helpful for exploring and better for immersion.
When the needed information is unrelated to the environment, head-stabilized display might be better. WHY?
Another reason for head-stabilized display is that it is hard to orient information when moving around.
World-stabilized information might even impact acquisition of information, since the information is not always in the same spatial orientation (e.g. i want info on oxygen, i look to the left).

Manufacturers of AR and VR headsets recommend avoiding classic 2D interfaces, because it breaks immersion. The planned use cases for this HUD require the opposite—to be able to focus on the primary task in the real world.

Apart from built-in HUDs, information can be presented via a head-mounted display. This affixes the display to the head, usually with a helmet or as glasses, enabling mobile usage. The display can cover one (monocular) or both eyes. Depending on the technology, both eyes receive the same image (biocular) or each will be shown a different image (binocular).

HMDs can be monocular, biocular or binocular. In a monocular system, both eyes receive different information, since the HMD is placed in front of only one eye. This introduces the problem of binocular rivalry. With a biocular display, the same image is presented to both eyes, which affects depth perception. Binocular presents a different image to both eyes CAKMAKCI 2006. The depth perception here is affected by the vergence-accomodation conflict. also causes eye strain.

\subsection{Optical See-Through Displays}

There are two options to combine digital content with reality. With video see-through, information will be superimposed on a video stream filmed by a camera on the headset. These headsets are fully occluded, the stream replaces the outside view.
An optical see-through system allows the user to directly view reality. Optical combiners

There is no weighing of advantages here. When a video see-through system fails, it will be impossible to see. Failing equipment must not impair vision, especially because it cannot be taken off in a fully encapsulated suit. Other advantages include .
	- cheaper than VST, better resolution
	- no eye offset, which hinders depth perception and might cause motion sickness
	- FOV limitations are cause by distortions of optical tech

\textcite{syberfeldtAugmentedRealitySmart2017} puts the boundary for an acceptable field of view at 30° at minimum in industrial use cases, which current systems can fulfill. Microsoft's HoloLens sits at 43°x29°, a few systems hit around 40° (https://vr-compare.com/ar).
Retinal projection technology does not have limitations on FOV, but is still in development.

As with all head-worn devices, HMDs are suceptible to shaking when walking.
\textcite{janakaNotiFadeMinimizingOHMD2023} also found that movement causes legibility issues because of screen shake. 
Walking can cause the HMD to shake, which impacts legibility. This problem can be avoided by using fonts with thicker strokes (both horizontal and vertical) [\cite{matsuuraReadabilityLegibilityFonts2019}].

\subsubsection{Ambient Light and Contrast Issues}

Regardless of optic technology, images are formed by combining light, meaning the display competes with ambient lighting for visibility, which is especially problematic in outdoor conditions. Most established OSTs provide around X lux, compared to overcast winter sky ca. 6000 lux, sunlight > 100.000 lux.
The biggest issue for legibility is missing contrast in bright environments. Especially outdoors, visibility of current OST technology is poor. \textcite{kerrWearableMobileAugmented2011}, for example, report that participants repeatedly used their hands to shield the display from external light to be able to read  the content on the display.

This follows the additive color model, where adding all wavelengths of light together results in white. Black is defined throught the absence of light. With physical screens, the color we see is the actual color of the display material. Since OSTs have no physical screen, "black" is transparent [\cite{gabbardColorBlendingOutdoor2013}]. This also means that darker colors are unavailable for designing on OSTs, since they are mostly translucent. Many current OSTs like [MODEL] have shaded lenses to improve the contrast somewhat \cite{itohIndistinguishableAugmentedReality2021}. Unfortunately, current AR technology is not capable of rendering the contrast levels necessary for outdoor use [\cite{ericksonExploringLimitationsEnvironment2020}]. Technical solutions in development include OSTs that can partially occlude areas of the display to block light. Still very experimental [\cite{davariOcclusionManagementTechniques2020}].

TANAKA 2008 propose a method to automatically place content where the readability is better, i.e. darker segments of the background. Concepts like these might not be usable in safety-critical use cases, since the user relies on spatially anchored elements for fast acquisition of information.
