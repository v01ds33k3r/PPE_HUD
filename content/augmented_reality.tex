Going after AZUMA 1997's definition, for applications to be classified as AR they have to fulfill the following three requirements: they combine real and virtual content, they are interactive in real-time, and virtual objects are registered in 3D space (world-stabilized). A head-up display overlays information onto reality in the user's line of sight to avoid the need to look away from their primary task to acquire needed information. Originally used in military aircraft, today HUDs are built into consumer vehicles as well. The displayed information is not anchored to the real world. In the case of a head-worn display, the information is head-stabilized, i.e. 2D planes around the user (see fig AR layers).

\subsection{Optical See-Through Displays}

Apart from built-in HUDs, information can be presented via a head-mounted display. This affixes the display to the head, usually with a helmet or as glasses, enabling mobile usage. The display can cover one (monocular) or both eyes. Depending on the technology, both eyes receive the same image (biocular) or each will be shown a different image (binocular).

HMDs can be monocular, biocular or binocular. In a monocular system, both eyes receive different information, since the HMD is placed in front of only one eye. This introduces the problem of binocular rivalry. With a biocular display, the same image is presented to both eyes, which affects depth perception. Binocular presents a different image to both eyes CAKMAKCI 2006. The depth perception here is affected by the vergence-accomodation conflict. also causes eye strain.

\subsubsection{Ambient Light and Contrast Issues}

Regardless of optic technology, images are formed by combining light, meaning the display competes with ambient lighting for visibility, which is especially problematic in outdoor conditions. Most established OSTs provide around X lux, compared to overcast winter sky ca. 6000 lux, sunlight > 100.000 lux.

KERR 2011, for example, report that participants repeatedly used their hands to shield the display from external light to be able to read  the content on the display.

This follows the additive color model, where adding all wavelengths of light together results in white. Black is defined throught the absence of light. With physical screens, the color we see is the actual color of the display material. Since OSTs have no physical screen, "black" is transparent GABBARD, 2013. This also means that darker colors are unavailable for designing on OSTs, since they are mostly translucent.

Many current OSTs like [MODEL] have shaded lenses to improve the contrast somewhat ITOH 2021. Unfortunately, current AR technology is not capable of rendering the contrast levels necessary for outdoor use ERICKSON 2020. Technical solutions in development include OSTs that can partially occlude areas of the display to block light. Still very experimental DAVARI 2020.

(On the other hand, when wearing an OST in dark environments, it is advisable to adjust the brightness to avoid eye strain and washing out the real world.)

The biggest issue for legibility is missing contrast in bright environments. Especially outdoors, visibility of current OST technology is poor.
