\section{Augmented Reality}
\label{sec:AR}

Augmented reality (AR) is a subcategory of extended reality (XR), alongside mixed reality (MR) and virtual reality (VR). In AR, virtual objects are "superimposed upon or composited with the real world" [\cite{azumaSurveyAugmentedReality1997}], in opposition to virtual reality which—by replacing the real environment entirely—creates the deepest sense of immersion. Mixed reality combines features of both concepts, the main distinction being the ability to interact with virtual objects in the real world [\cite{harichandanaExploringFrontiersUser2023}].

\textcite{azumaSurveyAugmentedReality1997} defines three requirements for applications to be classified as AR. They combine real and virtual content, they are interactive in real-time, and the virtual objects are registered in 3D space. With the technological advancements since the 2000s these requirements are understood more as guidelines rather than rules.

Head-up displays (HUDs) overlay information onto reality in the user's line of sight, allowing them to access necessary information without having to look away from their primary task.
Originally used in military aircraft, today HUDs are installed into consumer vehicles as well. In addition to these integrated HUDs, content can be presented on a head-mounted display. In this case, the display is attached to the head, typically as a helmet or glasses, enabling mobile usage. In contrast to the above definition by \textcite{azumaSurveyAugmentedReality1997}, the content displayed on a HUD is two-dimensional and head-stabilized, rather than spatially anchored, i.e., world-stabilized (see Figure \ref{fig:XR_layers}). Strictly speaking, a HUD therefore does not meet the criteria for AR. However, other research considers head-stabilized presentation of content as suitable for augmented reality [\cite{feinerWindowsWorld2D1993}, \cite{luGlanceableAREvaluating2020}].

\textcite{linFindingOptimumPosition2021} also point out that world-stabilized graphics are rarely used in industrial applications, particularly in full-shift scenarios where the HMD would have to be worn continuously. Furthermore, work tasks often require an overview of the situation rather than spatially distributed information. Additionally, with a 2D HUD, elements are always displayed in the same location, making it easier to find the needed information.

\subsection{Optical See-Through Displays}
\label{subsec:OST}

There are two approaches to combining virtual content with reality. With video see-through displays, information is superimposed onto a video stream captured by a camera mounted on the device. These headsets fully enclose the head to block external light. In contrast, an optical see-through (OST) system allows the user to view reality directly. Here, the overlay is created by optical combiners blending light from the image source and the environment together.

Since video see-through displays completely block the view of the outside world, it is impossible to see anything in the event of a failure. Given the intended use cases, this represents a major safety issue, especially since the headset cannot be removed while wearing a fully encapsulating suit.

\subsubsection{Ambient Light and Contrast Issues}

Regardless of the optic technology used, images are created by combining light, which means the display has to compete with ambient light for visibility. This results in a significant loss of contrast, especially outdoors.
\textcite{kerrWearableMobileAugmented2011}, for example, report that participants repeatedly used their hands to shield the display from external light to be able to read its contents.

Many manufacturers of optical see-through displays equip them with shaded lenses to improve the contrast somewhat [\cite{itohIndistinguishableAugmentedReality2021}]. Unfortunately, current OST technology is incapable of producing the contrast levels required for outdoor use [\cite{ericksonExploringLimitationsEnvironment2020}]. 
Technical solutions currently in development include OSTs that can partially occlude areas of the display to block light. However, these are still very experimental [\cite{kiyokawaOpticalSeethroughDisplay2000}, \cite{davariOcclusionManagementTechniques2020}].

As a hardware-independent solution, \cite{tanakaInformationLayoutMethod2008} propose a method for automatically placing content where readability is better, i.e. darker segments of the background. Such concepts might not be suitable for safety-critical scenarios, as users rely on spatially anchored elements to quickly acquire information.

\subsubsection{Color Blending}

In the additive color model, the combination of all wavelengths of light produce white, while black is created by the absence of light. When physical screens display black, the corresponding pixels do not emit light, so the actual color of the display material is visible. Since OHMDs do not have a physical screen, "black" is transparent [\cite{gabbardColorBlendingOutdoor2013}]. This also means that darker colors are not available for design on OHMDs, as they are mostly translucent. Additionally, the level of transparency affects the extent to which the background distorts the color [\cite{itohIndistinguishableAugmentedReality2021}].

\textcite{gabbardColorBlendingOutdoor2013} additionally found that, depending on the background, colors may shift in hue or washout due to their luminance value and/or chromaticity. Both of these effects result in colors potentially being misinterpretated or not being visible at all.
