\section{Augmented Reality}
\label{sec:AR}

Augmented reality (AR) is a subcategory of extended reality (XR), next to mixed reality (MR) and virtual reality (VR). In AR virtual objects are "superimposed upon or composited with the real world" [\cite{azumaSurveyAugmentedReality1997}], in opposition to virtual reality which—by replacing the real environment entirely—creates the deepest sense of immersion. Mixed reality combines features of both, the main distinction being the ability to interact with virtual objects in the real world [\cite{harichandanaExploringFrontiersUser2023}].

\textcite{azumaSurveyAugmentedReality1997} defines three requirements for applications to be classified as AR. They combine real and virtual content, they are interactive in real-time, and the virtual objects are registered in 3D space. With the technological advancements since the 2000s these requirements are understood more as guidelines than rules.

A head-up display overlays information onto reality in the user's line of sight to avoid the need to look away from their primary task to acquire needed information. Originally used in military aircraft, today HUDs are built into consumer vehicles as well. Apart from these integrated HUDs, content can be presented on a head-worn display. This affixes the display to the head, typically as a helmet or glasses, enabling mobile usage.  Contrary to the above definition by \textcite{azumaSurveyAugmentedReality1997}, the content displayed on a HUD is two-dimensional and head-stabilized, rather than anchored spatially, i.e. world-stabilized (see figure \ref{fig:XR_layers}). Nevertheless, some authors, such as \textcite{luGlanceableAREvaluating2020}, categorize HUDs as AR.

\textbf{Field of View.} \textcite{syberfeldtAugmentedRealitySmart2017} sets the threshold for an acceptable field of view at a minimum of 30° for industrial applications. Microsoft's HoloLens 2 features a 43° by 29° field of view, while a few other systems reach around 40°.\footnote{\href{https://vr-compare.com/ar}{Compare all AR Headsets \& Glasses - VRcompare}}

\textbf{Screen Shake.} As with all head-worn devices, HMDs are susceptible to shaking when moving, which causes legibility issues [\cite{janakaNotiFadeMinimizingOHMD2023}]. Especially thin, horizontal lines tend to disappear optically due to the up- and down movement.

\subsection{Optical See-Through Displays}

There are two approaches for combining virtual content with reality. With video see-through, information will be superimposed on a video stream filmed by a camera on the headset. These headsets fully occlude sight, the stream replaces the outside view. An optical see-through system allows the user to directly view reality.

\subsubsection{Ambient Light and Contrast Issues}

Regardless of the optic technology used, images are created by combining light, which means the display has to compete with ambient light for visibility. This results in a significant loss of contrast, especially outdoors.
% Most established OSTs provide around X lux, compared to overcast winter sky ca. 6000 lux, sunlight > 100.000 lux.
\textcite{kerrWearableMobileAugmented2011}, for example, report that participants repeatedly used their hands to shield the display from external light to be able to read  the its contents.

 Many manufacturers add shaded lenses to improve the contrast somewhat [\cite{itohIndistinguishableAugmentedReality2021}]. Unfortunately, current AR technology is not capable of rendering the contrast levels necessary for outdoor use [\cite{ericksonExploringLimitationsEnvironment2020}]. Technical solutions in development include OSTs that can partially occlude areas of the display to block light. Still very experimental [\cite{davariOcclusionManagementTechniques2020}].

On physical screens, the color we see corresponds to the actual color of the display material. Since OSTs do not have a physical screen, “black” is transparent [\cite{gabbardColorBlendingOutdoor2013}]. This also means that darker colors are not available for design on OSTs, as they are mostly translucent. Many current OSTs, such as [MODEL], feature tinted lenses to slightly improve contrast [\cite{itohIndistinguishableAugmentedReality2021}]. Unfortunately, current AR technology is unable to display the contrast levels required for outdoor use [\cite{ericksonExploringLimitationsEnvironment2020}]. Technical solutions currently under development include OSTs that can partially cover areas of the display to block light. However, this is still very experimental [\cite{davariOcclusionManagementTechniques2020}].

\subsubsection{Color Blending}

In the additive color model, the combination of all wavelengths of light produce white, while black is created by the absence of light. When physical screens are turned off, the actual color of the display material is visible. Since OSTs do not have a physical screen, "black" is transparent and darker colors mostly translucent [\cite{gabbardColorBlendingOutdoor2013}]. Additionally, the level of transparency affects the extent to which the background distorts the color [\cite{itohIndistinguishableAugmentedReality2021}].

\subsection{Methods of Interaction} %\subsection{Interaction and the Concept of Glanceability}

Due to not being constrained to two dimensions, XR applications can explore new types of interaction, such as gesture recognition, speech recognition and gaze tracking.
However, industrial use cases require hands-free interaction methods. Additionally, noise levels in these environments limit possible interaction to gaze tracking.
Even with these constraints, \textcite{harichandanaExploringFrontiersUser2023} emphasize the importance of interactions being intuitive and feeling natural. % -> again, decreasing cognitive burden

Gaze-based interaction is enabled by eye tracking technology. In addition to tracking eye position, it analyzes where the user is looking and how long.
