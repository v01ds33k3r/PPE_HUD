\subsection{The Field of View and Peripheral Vision}

The field of view spans approximately 200° to 220° horizontally and around 125° vertically, with the vertical field of view extending slightly further downward [\cite{loschkyScenePerceptionCentral2017}]. The central vision covers about 5° around the point of fixation, where acuity is highest [\cite{rosenholtzCapabilitiesLimitationsPeripheral2016}, \cite{loschkyScenePerceptionCentral2017}]. The remaining field is the periphery, with boundaries at 30° and 60°, dividing it into near, mid and far peripheral vision.

\begin{figure}[hbt]
    \centering

    \caption{Simplified illustration of the horizontal field of view.}
    \label{fig:FOV}
\end{figure}

Despite visual acuity and other functions deteriorating in the periphery, the area in front of us continues to appear in high resolution, with high contrast and color saturation [\cite{loschkyScenePerceptionCentral2017}]. According to \textcite{rosenholtzCapabilitiesLimitationsPeripheral2016}, the decline in performance of the peripheral vision is smaller than commonly assumed. A scene does not require many details to still allow object recognition and orientation, which is why image thumbnails remain recognizable despite their small size.

In addition, peripheral vision is particularly well suited for detecting motion, provides better vision in low-light conditions [\cite{johnsonChapter5Our2014}] and contributes to functions like orientation, navigation, balance, and hand-eye coordination. A restriction of the peripheral visual field severely impairs the performance of these functions, the effect increasing with the amount of vision obstructed [\cite{alfanoRestrictingFieldView1990}].

Color sensitivity decreases toward the periphery, red-green moreso than blue-yellow colors and sensitivity to luminance. \textcite{hansenColorPerceptionIntermediate2009} found that color perception is possible even at an eccentricity of 50°, provided the stimulus is sufficiently large. Most modern OST-HMDs feature a 40° field of view (see Section \ref{subsec:OST}), meaning the entire available color spectrum can be utilized.

\textcite{rosenholtzCapabilitiesLimitationsPeripheral2016} suggests that crowding is the biggest issue in parsing information located in the periphery. When visual elements are placed too close together, they blend into a single entity, making it harder to distinguish them from one another. This effect is more pronounced if the target and its neighboring elements are similar (e.g., same color and shape) [\cite{pelliUncrowdedWindowObject2008}]. The farther elements are from the center of vision, the greater the distance between them must be.

\subsection{Visual Attention}
\label{subsec:vis_attention}

% perception: preattentive and attentive mode
% habituation: less attention to any stimulus that occurs frequently

The retina transmits too much information to be processed all at once. This bottle neck is resolved by visual attention, which focuses resources on just a fragment of the current visual stimuli [\cite{andersonDirectedVisualAttention2005}, \cite{rosenholtzCapabilitiesLimitationsPeripheral2016}].

Two processes determine where this attention is focused next. Bottom-up attention occurs involuntarily, triggered by external stimuli. 
In the top-down process, the gaze is directed toward objects related to current needs or goals. Interestingly, top-down attention reduces the impact of distracting stimuli while consciously focusing on a visual target [\cite{connorVisualAttentionBottomUp2004}].
