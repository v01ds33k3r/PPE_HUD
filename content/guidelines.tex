\section{Design Guidelines}
\label{sec:guidelines}

Following scenario requirements, technical limitations and human factors, the HUD needs to fulfill the following demands:

Ideally, the finished system shows as much information as possible/necessary without limiting situation awareness, causing fatigue or sensory overload. The displayed information should be comprehensible in as short a time as possible.

In an emergency, the system must be able to get the user's attention, without hindering decision-making or interrupting the primary task completely.

Referencing rules for visual hierarchy, the usage of contrasts heightens difference between the two modes by limiting color and movement to emergency functions to get and keep attention without hindering reaction times.

The following design guidelines are tailored to ensure the best visibility and information acquisition possible.

\subsection{Color and Contrast}

The use of color is mainly limited by current optical see-through technology. As mentioned by GABBARD, colors with high source luminance will be visible best. Tying into economic reasons, designing the HUD functions to work with one color only ensures affordability as well.

Limiting the UI to one color makes sure the design works in all aspects (e.g. using motion to get attention before color). Another reason is the economic factor: especially in scenarios where it's likely that equipment needs to be replaced because of damage or contamination, the acquisition cost should be as low as possible. One way to achieve this is to only have a monochrome display. So, the HUD UI should be able to function without accent colors. This also avoids impairing users with color blindness.

The library chroma.js can sort colors by luminance in the RGB color space, based on the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines 2.0. Sorting the base colors red (\#FF0000), green (\#00FF00), blue (\#0000FF) and their intermediaries cyan (\#00FFFF), magenta (\#FF00FF) and yellow (\#FFFF00) puts them into the following order (darkest to brightest): blue, red, magenta, green, cyan and yellow.

\subsection{Typography and Stroke Width}

Aside from screen shake, reading performance is further impacted by lighting and background texture. To find requirements for font size, RENKEWITZ 2008 tested the effect of "background texture and font color on text recognition". They set a benchmark of two seconds as threshold for fast recognition, which resulted in font size thresholds from 23 to 42 pt, depending on color and background. These numbers might have to be adjusted for an OST-HMD, since they used a video see-through system, and red and blue as colors, both colors with lower luminance.

The typeface "Inter" fulfills all of the requirements. It has a uniform thickness of strokes, square letter width and good spacing between its lines.

Naturally, the same requirements exist for the stroke width.

\subsection{Layout and Spacing}

As mentioned in PERIPHERAL VISION, crowding affects information acquisition the most. It seems advisable to prefer wider spacing between elements. limiting the number of elements per side to 3 so they can be spatially anchored to the corners and one slot in between.

\cite{chuaPositioningGlassInvestigating2016}
- ! monocular OST-HMD
- middle + bottom center positions were noticed quicker
- top + peripheral positions were more comfortable, unobtrusive
- center-right is mentioned as best balance between usability + comfort

\cite{katsuyamaEffectsVisualDisplay1989}
- studied task requiring frequent eye shifts
- (1) displays located 15° below a primary viewing area are better perceived than identical displays located 15° above
- (2) ratings of discomfort involved in eye movements (necessitated by the viewing of physically separated displays) correspond to their performances on the secondary task
- task performance decreases the further the distance from center

\cite{kimInfluencesAugmentedReality2019}
- tasks that require referencing information
- "performance, workload, and usability were more affected by UI designs than the HWD type."
- "Compared to the baseline paper pick list condition, using an HWD, especially with the graphic-based UI, led to improved job performance and reduced perceived workload, while causing increased discomfort"
- "Responses to the workplace cognitive failure scale also indicated a lower risk of cognitive failure specifically with the graphic-based UI design + always-on display."

DYSON 2001

According to \textcite{linFindingOptimumPosition2021}, 8° to 20° horizontally seem to be most comfortable.
Vertically limit to 8° above and below visual center
- "We are surprised to discover that there are no significant differences in task times between the four different display positions. We expected a more uncomfortable viewing angle would have a more dramatic effect in slowing users so that their order picking times would increase, especially for the bottom-right position." BUT "we realized that users stop after picking one book to find and plan their path to pick their next book, instead of trying to read and interpret data while on the move. The extra time taken to stop and plan their path could mask the differences in time taken to perceive picking information"
- "the bottom-center position could be inferior in situations in which users have to look at their feet such as stairs mobility and rough terrain maneuvering."
- "A bottom-right display could also interfere with tasks involving the hands."
- "The display situated between 5.4° to 14.6° toward the ear was most preferred by users. It did not block the line-of-sight view and was also closest to center-center positioning. However, Dowell’s work on cognitive capture [8] suggests keeping all pixels at least 8° from line-of-sight."
- "there may be an ideal, general purpose HWD position between 8 to 20° temporally offset from line-of-sight. Horizontal FOV would thus be 12°. Vertical FOV requires more investigation, but limiting pixels to between ±8° from center as in Haynes’s comfort study [21] seems wise"
- "As mentioned previously, from Chua’s work we wish to avoid nasally offset displays, and positions much above line-of-sight are uncomfortable for extended use CHUA 2015."
