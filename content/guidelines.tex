\section{Design Guidelines}
\label{sec:guidelines}

The following sections summarize the scenario requirements, technical limitations and human factors.

% The following design guidelines are tailored to ensure the best visibility and information acquisition possible.

Ideally, the HUD shows as much information as possible without limiting situation awareness, causing fatigue or sensory overload. The displayed information should be comprehensible in as short a time as possible.

In an emergency, the system must be able to get the user's attention, without hindering decision-making or interrupting the primary task completely.

\subsection{Color and Contrast}

As mentioned in Section \ref{sec:AR}, the use of color is mainly limited by current optical see-through technology. \textcite{gabbardPerceptualColorMatchingMethod2022} advises to prefer colors with high source luminance, since these are less subject to color blending. Further, if a color usually signifies a specific meaning and cannot be guaranteed to be perceived correctly, "designers should instead consider other color(s) that exhibit higher luminance but are still meaningfully interpretable". They specifically mention red as such an example.

The library chroma.js\footnote{\href{https://gka.github.io/chroma.js/\#color-luminance}{chroma.js api docs!}} has a funtion to sort colors by luminance in the RGB color space, based on the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines 2.0.\footnote{\href{https://www.w3.org/TR/2008/REC-WCAG20-20081211/\#relativeluminancedef}{Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.0}} Sorting the base colors red (\#FF0000), green (\#00FF00), blue (\#0000FF) and their intermediaries cyan (\#00FFFF), magenta (\#FF00FF), and yellow (\#FFFF00) puts them into the following order (brightest to darkest): yellow, cyan, green, magenta, red, and blue.

Limiting the UI to a single color can ensure that the design works effectively in every respect (e.g., by using motion to draw attention instead of color). This also offers economic benefits, as in scenarios where devices are likely to need replacement due to damage or contamination, acquisition costs should be kept as low as possible. One way to ensure affordability is the usage of a monochrome display. This further avoids impairing users with color blindness.

\subsection{Typography}

% text drawing styles

Aside from screen shake, reading performance is impacted by lighting and background texture, reiterating the importance of proper contrast.

To find requirements for font size, \textcite{renkewitzOptimalFontSize2008} tested to what extent background font colors affect text recognition. They set a benchmark of two seconds as threshold for fast recognition, which resulted in recommended font sizes from 23 to 42 pt, depending on color and background. However, these numbers might have to be adjusted for an OST-HMD, since they used a video see-through system, and red and blue as colors, both colors with lower luminance.

As explained in \ref{sec:AR}, thinner lines disappear when the HMD moves.
\textcite{matsuuraReadabilityLegibilityFonts2019} confirm that heavier font weights improve readability. Naturally, the same requirements exist for stroke width.

For example, the typeface "Inter" fits the requirements well. It has a uniform thickness of strokes, a square letter form and good spacing between its lines.

\subsection{Iconography}

% "Well-designed graphical icons can achieve such positive effects as drawing users’ attention, shortening search time, reducing operational errors, and relieving users’ burden." \cite{linStudyRelationshipsDifferent2016}

% Needed information is found quicker when presented as graphical content.
% Icons can utilize small screen estate more efficently.

% - amount of coded information

\textcite{janakaCanIconsOutperform2023} tested if, and under which conditions icons perform better than text.
Icon-augmented notifications enabled significantly higher primary task performance than text only and reduced the level of interruption while maintaining reaction and comprehension. They also found icons are more comprehensible when task complexity increased or demanded more attention. The notification were "easier to perceive in suboptimal conditions", including outdoors when walking.

Naturally, this requires the icons to be well-designed. To that end, \textcite{linStudyRelationshipsDifferent2016} tested versions of icons against each other. The differences tested were polarity, plane- and shape-based, and outline or not. Since line-based icons dissect the space more often, adding visual complexity, which lowers recognition performance. Additionally, white on black icons performed best. 

Another reason to prefer shape-based icons is the requirement of thicker line widths which might impact an icon's readability. Simpler icons with varied silhouettes offset this.

\subsection{Layout and Spacing}

As mentioned in Section \ref{sec:HVS_attention}, crowding affects perception the most. It seems advisable to prefer wider spacing between elements. 

Additionally, the number of components per side should be limited to three at most, anchoring them spatially to the corners and sides of the display and keeping the center of vision free.
